Hinge Data Analysis: Impact of comments on Matches

Hinge is uniquely different from other dating platforms because it allows users to add a comment on the like they send. This extra feature considerably increases the degree of freedom a user can exert to increase the chances of a match. In this report I will be analyzing my past year Hinge data to find out if comments influence my matches and find out optimal comment strategy for future likes.

3. Confounding variable in time

The above analysis assumes all likes in a particular category are equivalent. However, there might be additional confounding variables. One likely confoundding variable can be some trend in time..

Clearly we can see that there is a period between July and August where both matches and likes considerably increase. This is the period I was in India and using Hinge with paid subscription. I highlight this period in Gold above. In the rest of the period I am using hinge in Europe/UK.

To see the impact of Subscription, below I show the match success rate for likes given in the subscription period vs those given outside.

We can see that subscription roughly doubles the match success rate. So it does not make sense to simply combine the data in all the periods to obtain the true success rate of a comment.

To separate out the effect of the subscription period and obtain true success rate of each comments we will need to make some model assumptions and perform more careful analysis.

7. Bayesian Analysis

7.1 Generalized Linear Model

Here we will use a Bayesian approach to estimate the true success rate for the comments. I model the success probability, $p$, of a $i$ like I send, through

$$ p = \text{sigmoid}(\alpha + \beta \cdot \text{has\_comment} + \sum_{i\neq \text{random}} \beta_i \cdot \text{cluster}_i + B \cdot \text{in\_subscription}) $$

  • Here the Sigmoid is a function that takes a number from between $-\infty$ to $\infty$ to a number between $0$ and 1. This ensures that our probability stays positive and smaller than 1.
  • has_comment is 1 if I have a comment in the like I am sending, else has_comment is 0.
  • in_subscription is 1 if the like I am sending is during the subscription period, else it is 0.
  • $\text{cluster}_i$ is 1 if the comment in the like belongs to $i^{th}$ cluster. The sum is over all clusters except the cluster belonging to random comments (C7).
  • $\alpha$, $\beta$, $\beta_i$, and $B$ are the model parameters that we will estimate using the data

Implicit Model Assumptions

  1. All likes sent have a base matching probability determined by $\alpha$
  2. All likes with a comment get a boost in probability determined by $\beta$
  3. $\beta_i$ encodes the relative change in probability if a comment belongs to a specific $i^{th}$ cluster we identified before. Because comments in the 'random' cluster are hard to group together, we do not assign them a special $\beta_i$ and instead their probability are determined by the common $\beta$.
  4. All likes get the same boost, $B$, in the subscription period regardless of what comment we send

By fitting the above model on the data we get posterior distributions on the moddel parameters, $\alpha$, $\beta$, etc. However, the estimates of just these parameters are harder to interpret and are also not what we actually care about.

Since I am interested in finding what actions we should take in the future, below I plot the relative increase in matching probability for different action choices, relative to the probability of sending like with random comment wihtout subscription. For e.g. for likes sent without comment we have, $$ \%\text{Relative probability}= \frac{\text{Sigmoid}(\alpha)-\text{Sigmoid}(\alpha+\beta)}{\text{Sigmoid}(\alpha+\beta)}\times 100 $$

Conclusions: The only statistically significant conclusions we can draw is that we should avoid sending 'hey trouble' comment and to use subscription to increase future matching rate. The canned comments show decent superiority over generic comments and I should get more data on them to confirm their superiority.

7.2 (Optional) Beta Hierarchical Model

The previous model was one way we can model the matching rate. But since we do not know the exact theory we would like to avoid any systematic issues due to our modelling choices. So here I use a very different model to fit the data

I start by assuming that the matching probabilities of different choices (subscription, special comments, no comments, etc) are drawn from a common distribution: Beta Distribution with unknown parameters $\alpha$ and $\beta$. This base distribution is assumed to be determined by my Hinge profile and hence affects the success rate of all likes I send out.

Specifically, the Beta distribution determines the average success probability of any like I send out: $$\bar{p}=\frac{\alpha}{\alpha+\beta}$$.

To model the influence of subscription, I assume that having subscription shifts my whole Beta distribution, such that $$\bar{p}_{sub}=\text{max}(1,(1+B)\bar{p}),$$ where $B$ is the boost due to subscription.

Future systematic checks

There are several other systematics that were not accounted for in the analysis.

  1. My hinge account itself evolves over time. Hinge data does not have a history of my photo updates. I can log the dates of my photo updates and account for it in future data.
  2. The probability of match success likely changes for girls with different Elo ratings. My succes of like is lower on a girl with much higher rating. Hinge ofcourse does not shore the rating of girl for every like. In future I plan on putting my rating of a girl in the comments I send out.
